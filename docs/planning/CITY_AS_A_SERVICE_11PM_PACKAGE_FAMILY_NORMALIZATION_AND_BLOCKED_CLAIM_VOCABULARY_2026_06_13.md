## Why normalization matters now

The AAS plan stack has two useful vocabularies that should not be collapsed into one another:

1. **Package-family language** — conservative internal/admin terms like `Bounded Local Count`, `Visible Asset State Snapshot`, and `Compliance Hold Review`. These are safe while the system is paused because they describe bounded uncertainty patterns and blocked claims.
2. **Offer/catalog language** — commercial labels like `Counter Reality Check`, `Packet Submission Attempt`, and `Posting Compliance Check`. These are useful for future packaging, but they are higher-risk because they can sound like sellable promises, dispatchable work, or customer-facing commitments.

Tonight's safest contribution is to keep both vocabularies connected while making the boundary explicit. Future planning can then reuse older catalog material without accidentally implying public launch, pricing approval, worker instructions, or collection authorization.

## Naming rules for future docs

1. **Lead with canonical internal family names.** Use offer labels only as examples or future aliases.
2. **Append posture when relevant.** Prefer `Bounded Local Count — held`, `Visible Asset State Snapshot — concept-only`, or `Compliance Hold Review — internal/admin` over launch-sounding names.
3. **Do not put price, SLA, dispatch, queue, or customer promise language in a title** unless a separate explicit authorization exists.
4. **Keep `safe_claim` names internal.** Safe claims should describe landed planning artifacts, not business readiness.
5. **Keep the answer gate visible.** Any Bounded Local Count work must state whether exactly one operator-approved value exists. Current answer state: none observed.
