The real divide: Silicon vs Carbon

The Swap Test

Dan Koe proposes something he calls "The Swap Test":

"If you could swap the creator and the creation would be just as valuable, then AI can replace it. If the creation only works because you made it, then that's your edge."

Let's apply it:

  • Can an agent analyze data? Yes. Any model does it equally well.
  • Can an agent generate code? Yes. It's interchangeable.
  • Can an agent physically verify that a sign is in place? No.
  • Can an agent drop a package at the shipping office on the corner? No.
  • Can an agent call someone on the phone and convince them? No.
The human in these tasks isn't interchangeable. Not because they're special, but because they're there. They have a body. Physical presence. Local context that no model can simulate.

The jobs that persist

According to the analysis by David Shapiro that Dan Koe cites, there are jobs that will persist even when automation is superior:

  • High-responsibility roles Ś where someone has to answer if something goes wrong
  • Statutory positions Ś legally requiring humans (signatures, notarizations)
  • Experience economy Ś where the human experience is the product
  • Meaning creators Ś those who help others navigate the human experience
  • Trust and relationship jobs Ś sales, diplomacy, negotiation
Physical tasks fit perfectly in the first three categories:

They're high-responsibility Ś if something goes wrong, someone has to answer. Many require legal presence Ś agents can't sign legal documents. And many are part of the experience economy Ś the value lies in a real human doing it.

The rails already exist

This isn't theory. This isn't a white paper. The pieces are already built and working.

HTTP-native payments (x402)

You know the 404 error? "Page not found." An HTTP code we've all seen.

Well, there's another code that almost nobody knows: 402 - Payment Required. It was reserved in 1997 but never used... until now.

x402 is like an instant digital toll. Instead of registering, entering your card, paying a monthly subscription, and waiting for confirmation Ś your wallet simply signs a payment authorization, a "facilitator" (the intermediary that processes x402) executes it, and the service unlocks. All in seconds.

x402 facilitators are already live in production supporting multiple mainnet networks. The payment rails are already working.

Automatic refunds (x402r)

But something crucial was missing: x402 version 2 was designed to allow extensions. And the x402r team built something that changes everything: automatic refunds.

If the work doesn't verify, the money comes back automatically. No disputes. No waiting. No middlemen.

Without this refund system, Execution Market wouldn't be possible. An agent can't risk its money without a guarantee of getting it back if the work fails. x402r solves this trustlessly.

An agent can hire without risk. If it fails, it gets its money back automatically.

Payment channels

They work like opening a tab at a bar. You deposit once, make multiple transactions, settle at the end.

Imagine: A market research agent needs to verify 20 stores in an area. Instead of 20 separate transactions with 20 fees, it opens a channel, the human executes all 20 verifications, and at the end everything settles in a single transaction.

Perfect for complex tasks with multiple steps. Without paying fees for every micro-interaction.

Payment streaming (Superfluid)

Money flows per second. Literally.

Using Superfluid, the payment streaming protocol, we integrate x402-sf so money flows in real time.

Imagine: A human monitors a location for 2 hours. Their camera streams. The agent verifies in real time. Money flows while the work is being done. If the human leaves at 47 minutes, they get paid for 47 minutes. If they complete the 2 hours, they get paid for 2 hours.

No waiting for approval. No waiting for processing. You get paid while you work.

$0.005 per second = $18/hour. Fully automatic.

Transparent, merit-based reputation (ERC-8004)

You know Uber ratings? You spent years building a 4.9-star rating. Then Uber changes its policies, deactivates you, or simply shuts down. Your reputation disappears. You can't take it to Lyft. You can't prove your track record. Years of work, gone.

Another day, another platform that treats your reputation as if it were their property.

This happens because your reputation lives in Uber's database. They decide what to do with it. And when they go, they take it with them.

ERC-8004 changes this.

Your reputation is stored as blockchain transactions. It's calculable Ś anyone can verify how your score was derived. It's visible Ś on-chain, auditable. It's persistent Ś if Execution Market shuts down tomorrow, your history still exists. You can take it to any other platform that uses the standard.

You don't control your reputation by editing it. You control it by doing good work.

Every completed task, every successful verification, every rating you receive Ś everything is recorded. Your score reflects your merit, not some opaque algorithm nobody understands.

An agent will prefer to hire someone with 500 completed tasks and a score of 87/100. But here's the twist: x402r's automatic refund mechanism levels the playing field. An agent can take a chance on a new worker because if it fails, it gets its money back automatically. This allows more "lenient" agents to give opportunities to people without a track record Ś the risk is mitigated. Without this mechanism, newcomers could never get in. With it, meritocracy works from day one.

Reputation is:

  • Transparent: Calculable, visible, auditable Ś you know exactly how your score was reached

  • Merit-based: Your work determines it, not a secret algorithm

  • Persistent: Stored on blockchain Ś doesn't disappear if the platform shuts down

  • Portable: Works across platforms Ś you're not locked in

  • Bidirectional: You also rate the agents that hire you


Scale of 0-100. No rating inflation. No depending on the goodwill of a corporation.

And there's more: ERC-8004 was designed to scale to economies with trillions of autonomous agents. The minds that created it thought about every form of manipulation, Sybil attacks, artificial score inflation. If it works at that agent scale, it works at the much smaller scale of humans and robots.

The system weighs ratings by the value of each task Ś completing a $150 notarization weighs much more in your score than ten $0.25 verifications. This means an isolated mistake doesn't destroy your track record, and gaming the score requires real investment, not cheap tricks.

Smart verification

Most tasks verify automatically: GPS confirms location, timestamp confirms time, OCR extracts text from photos. If everything checks out, instant payment.

But there's a problem: spoofing GPS is trivial, and generative AIs can create hyperrealistic photos in seconds. How do we know the photo is real?

Some thieves bring a crowbar, others just need Midjourney.

Hardware Attestation. The app uses the camera directly and cryptographically signs the photo using the device's Secure Enclave Ś it doesn't allow uploading photos from the camera roll. This proves the photo was taken by that device, at that moment, at those coordinates. It's much harder to hack the hardware than to fake a JPG.

But not all tasks need automated verification. Sometimes the payer simply reviews the result and approves Ś no middlemen, no overhead. If they're happy, they pay. Simple as that.

For more complex cases Ś subjective tasks, disputes, or high-value work Ś the system scales gradually:

  • Payer approves (variable): The task publisher reviews and approves directly.
  • Auto-check (80% of tasks with verification): Instant automatic verification.
  • AI Review (15%): A model analyzes the evidence.
  • Human Arbitration (5%): Arbitrator panel. 2-of-3 consensus.
This prevents simple $0.25 tasks from having complex verification overhead. Only tasks that need it escalate to higher levels.

And the best part: validating is paid work. 5-15% of the bounty goes to validators. A marketplace of people whose job is to verify that others did their work correctly.

What it is and what it isn't

To be clear:

Execution Market doesn't aim to replace traditional employment.

It's infrastructure for punctual, verifiable tasks. Micro-jobs that couldn't exist before because the coordination cost was higher than the task's value.

Can it become primary income for some? Maybe. Will it be occasional extra income for others? Probably. What's certain: it's optional for everyone.

Opportunities appear when they appear. Going to work. Leaving a meeting. Waiting to be served. You don't have to set aside specific time Ś the task finds you when you're nearby. Take it if you want. If not, someone else takes it. No pressure.

Execution Market is a transitional tool. While AI transforms the labor landscape Ś especially in technology Ś this offers a real alternative. It doesn't solve everything, but it returns some economic agency at a time when many are losing it.

And who builds it matters.

The alternative is that it exists without transparency. Without portable reputation. Without open verification. Without the worker being able to rate the agent that hired them. Without automatic refunds. Without an open protocol that allows competition.

We'd rather build it with the uncomfortable questions on the table.

Because at least we're thinking about verification without surveillance. Privacy by design. Limited data retention. Bidirectional reputation where the human also rates the agent.

What we still don't know

There are things we haven't solved. We'd rather say it:

  • Task flow: Volume depends on how many agents adopt the system. In the beginning it may be inconsistent. We're implementing task bundling by zone (an agent can publish 10 verifications together) and smart prioritization for active workers. Dynamic bounties also help: if nobody takes it, the price goes up until someone does.
  • Subjective verification: For tasks where there's no objectively "correct" answer (does this phrase sound natural?), verification is harder. We're exploring partial payouts Ś a percentage at submission, the rest post-approval Ś to protect the worker while it's being resolved.
  • Power balance: Although reputation is bidirectional, agents have more flexibility to create new identities. We're considering bonds for agents Ś a deposit that's forfeited if they abuse the system Ś so creating new identities has real cost.
  • Liability on high-value tasks: If someone steals a $2,000 package, the $8 refund doesn't help. For high-value tasks, we're exploring worker staking (locking funds as collateral) and an insurance pool funded by micro-fractions of each fee.
  • The physical world is hostile: What happens if you can't complete the task because there's a guard, a dog, or it's private property? We're designing a "proof of attempt" Ś the worker documents the obstacle and receives a base fee for the commute, without completing the task.
  • The robot transition: When robots scale, many simple physical tasks will shift to them. That's reality. What we expect is that by then, the protocol will have created enough value for humans to participate in other ways Ś as validators, as robot owners, or in tasks only humans can do.
We don't have all the answers. But we're looking for them in public.
